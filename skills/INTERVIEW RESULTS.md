# Rescue Vehicle Management System
## Consolidated Interview Results

---

# Common Findings Across Agencies

## Vehicle Monitoring

All participating agencies maintain records related to:

- Vehicle information
- Assigned drivers
- Driver licenses
- Vehicle status
- Repair and maintenance activities

However, monitoring is still primarily conducted through:

- Paper-based records
- Logbooks
- Verbal communication
- Phone calls
- Text messages
- Group chats
- Physical vehicle inspection

No agency currently uses a single integrated system that combines:

- Vehicle information management
- Driver management
- Vehicle inspections
- Damage and defect reporting
- Repair history
- Preventive maintenance monitoring
- Dispatch monitoring

---

# Vehicle Inspection Practices

## How are vehicles inspected before operations?

### Common Findings

All agencies conduct vehicle inspections before deployment, although the level of formality varies.

### Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP)

Uses a paper-based **Motor Vehicle Daily Safety Checklist**.

Inspection Items:

- Battery
- Lights
- Oil
- Water
- Brakes
- Air
- Gas
- Engine
- Tires
- Power Steering
- Horn/Siren
- Directional Signals
- Hydraulic System
- Fire Pump

### Philippine National Police (PNP)

Drivers perform daily BLOWBAGETS inspections before vehicle use.

### City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office (CDRRMO)

Drivers and responders conduct routine vehicle checks before operations.

### City Health Office (CHO)

Drivers perform practical inspections while waiting for patient transport or emergency deployment.

# Vehicle Maintenance Management

## How are vehicle defects and maintenance concerns reported?

### Common Findings

Most agencies still depend on:

- Verbal reporting
- Phone calls
- Manual forms
- Paper records
- Maintenance logbooks

---

## Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP)

### Maintenance Workflow

```text
Driver
→ Logistics Officer
→ Chief Operations Officer
→ Repair Decision
→ Repair Activity
→ Vehicle Status Update
```

### Additional Findings

- Uses a Motor Vehicle Ledger Card.
- Reports vehicle status as either:
  - Serviceable
  - Unserviceable
- Major repairs may require LGU support.

---

## Philippine National Police (PNP)

### Maintenance Workflow

```text
Driver
→ Supply Office
→ Chief / Chief Operations Officer
→ Repair Decision
→ Repair Activity
```

### Additional Findings

- No formal damage reporting form.
- Maintenance concerns are usually reported verbally.
- Uses a Preventive Maintenance System (PMS) software.
- Oil changes are typically performed every 3,000–5,000 kilometers.

---

## City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office (CDRRMO)

### Maintenance Workflow

```text
Driver / Responder
→ Team Leader
→ Vehicle Manager
→ Office Management
→ Repair Activity
```

### Additional Findings

- Vehicle Manager monitors vehicle conditions.
- Personnel may be reassigned when vehicles become unavailable.
- Returning vehicles undergo:
  - Disinfection
  - Equipment replenishment

---

## City Health Office (CHO)

### Maintenance Workflow

```text
Driver
→ Dispatcher
→ Administrative Office
→ GSO Motorpool / Authorized Dealer
→ Repair Activity
```

### Additional Findings

- Minor repairs are handled internally.
- Major repairs are referred to the GSO Motorpool.
- Vehicles under warranty are sent directly to authorized dealerships.

---

# Preventive Maintenance Practices

## Do agencies follow preventive maintenance schedules?

### Common Findings

All agencies perform preventive maintenance, although schedules differ depending on vehicle type and agency practices.

### PNP

- Oil change every 3,000–5,000 kilometers.

### CHO

- Mileage-based maintenance.
- Common interval: every 5,000 kilometers.

### CDRRMO

- Regular preventive maintenance schedules are followed.

### BFP

- No uniform mileage standard for all vehicles.
- Ambulances follow maintenance schedules coordinated with LGU and CDRRMO personnel.

# Dispatch Operations

## How are vehicles dispatched during operations?

### Common Findings

Dispatch operations are generally handled manually.

Typical dispatch information recorded includes:

- Vehicle
- Driver
- Destination
- Date
- Time
- Mission or purpose
- Responders or passengers

---

## City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office (CDRRMO)

### Dispatch Authority

- Team Leader

### Vehicle Availability Monitoring

Monitored through:

- Group chats
- Driver updates
- Team leader coordination

### Challenges

- Simultaneous emergencies
- Delayed reporting of vehicle defects
- Lack of centralized monitoring

---

## City Health Office (CHO)

### Dispatch Authority

- Dispatcher

### Information Recorded

- Destination
- Vehicle
- Patient name
- Assigned driver
- Date
- Accompanying personnel

### Dispatch Monitoring

- Managed by the dispatcher.
- Records are usually updated after operations.

### Vehicle Return Procedure

- Vehicle disinfection

### Challenges

- Limited vehicles
- Limited drivers
- Simultaneous service requests

---

## Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP)

### Dispatch Monitoring

Vehicle availability is monitored through:

- Personnel awareness
- Manual coordination
- Operational updates

No dedicated dispatch monitoring software is used.

---

## Philippine National Police (PNP)

### Dispatch Monitoring

Vehicle deployment is monitored through:

- Verbal communication
- Coordination among officers

No dedicated dispatch monitoring software is used.

# Driver Assignment Practices

## How are drivers assigned to vehicles?

### BFP

Drivers are typically assigned to specific vehicles.

### PNP

Drivers are assigned according to operational requirements.

### CDRRMO

Personnel and drivers are commonly assigned based on locality and operational responsibility.

### CHO

Uses a rotating driver schedule.

- Two primary drivers
- 24/7 shifting arrangement
- Drivers may operate multiple vehicles when necessary

# Common Problems Identified

## 1. Paper-Based Processes

Most agencies still use manual records for:

- Vehicle inspections
- Maintenance records
- Dispatch logs

---

## 2. Fragmented Information

Vehicle-related information is scattered across:

- Logbooks
- Forms
- Group chats
- Text messages
- Verbal communication

---

## 3. Delayed Reporting

Common issues include:

- Late submission of damage reports
- Delayed updating of dispatch records
- Inconsistent maintenance documentation

---

## 4. Lack of Centralized Monitoring

Agencies cannot immediately determine:

- Available vehicles
- Deployed vehicles
- Vehicles under repair
- Vehicles due for maintenance
- Complete maintenance histories

---

## 5. Communication Gaps

According to GSO Motorpool personnel:

- Submitted damage descriptions are sometimes incomplete.
- Additional defects are often discovered during inspection.
- Maintenance requests may lack sufficient details for immediate assessment.

# Questions and Responses Summary

## Q1. How are vehicles dispatched during emergency operations?

**Response:**

Dispatching is performed manually by authorized personnel such as Team Leaders, Dispatchers, Operations Officers, or Chiefs depending on the agency.

---

## Q2. How is vehicle availability monitored?

**Response:**

Availability is monitored through:

- Logbooks
- Group chats
- Verbal communication
- Physical observation
- Manual records

No agency currently uses an integrated monitoring system.

---

## Q3. How are maintenance concerns reported?

**Response:**

Most maintenance concerns are reported through:

- Verbal communication
- Phone calls
- Written forms
- Maintenance logbooks

---

## Q4. How are drivers assigned?

**Response:**

Assignment practices vary:

- Permanent vehicle assignment (BFP)
- Operational assignment (PNP)
- Area-based assignment (CDRRMO)
- Rotational assignment (CHO)

---

## Q5. What happens when a vehicle returns from operation?

**Response:**

Common activities include:

- Vehicle inspection
- Vehicle cleaning
- Disinfection
- Equipment replenishment
- Updating operational records

# Key Insight for the Proposed System

The interviews revealed that vehicle maintenance management and dispatch monitoring are closely related but are currently handled through separate manual processes.

Different personnel are responsible for:

- Vehicle inspections
- Dispatch monitoring
- Maintenance management
- Driver coordination

As a result, information becomes fragmented across multiple records and communication channels.

These findings directly support the implementation of the following modules within the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System:

- Vehicle Management
- Driver Management
- Digital BLOWBAGETS Inspection
- Damage and Defect Reporting
- Repair Logging
- Preventive Maintenance Scheduling
- Dispatch Logging and Monitoring
- Vehicle Status Monitoring
- Reports and Notifications

Overall, the interview results validate the need for a centralized system capable of integrating vehicle information, maintenance monitoring, dispatch records, inspections, and reporting within a single platform tailored for rescue vehicle operations.
