**CHAPTER 1**

## **INTRODUCTION**

### **Project Context** 

Government and emergency vehicle fleets in many countries still rely on manual recording systems and disconnected documentation practices that create recurring gaps in vehicle readiness and maintenance accountability. Vinay M. G. et al. (2025) found that manual vehicle information processes produce inconsistent records and poor data accessibility, while Richard Gorham et al. (2025) noted that the absence of centralized vehicle and driver databases weakens long-term monitoring and record validation. Crespo del Castillo and Parlikad (2024) determined that inadequate maintenance scheduling increases the risk of unexpected vehicle failures and operational downtime, and Carlos Bolaños et al. (2022) reported that fleet environments without centralized dispatch coordination suffer from poor operational visibility and reduced accountability. Philippine-based research shows the same problems. Mimbala et al. (2024) observed that manual logbook-based vehicle monitoring in institutional settings leads to inaccurate records and data loss, and Gallega (2025) noted that organized preventive maintenance scheduling is necessary for sustaining vehicle reliability. Nerona et al. (2025) found that manual administrative reporting increases the difficulty of consolidating and retrieving operational records within local government, while Intal et al. (2019) noted that maintenance monitoring deteriorates when agencies rely on paper-based and disconnected workflows.  
In Calbayog City, four government agencies (the CDRRMO, BFP, CHO, and PNP) collectively operate ambulances, fire trucks, patrol cars, and other rescue vehicles deployed for emergency response across the city's barangays. Interviews conducted with officers from each agency confirmed that all four rely on manual, paper-based practices with no shared centralized system in place. The BFP documents daily vehicle inspections using a paper-based Motor Vehicle Daily Safety Checklist following the BLOWBAGETS protocol and records defect and maintenance history in a physical Motor Vehicle Ledger Card, while the CDRRMO and CHO depend on informal, driver-triggered defect reporting with no standardized daily inspection form, and the PNP communicates maintenance concerns verbally with no formal documentation at the station level. Fleet availability monitoring is no more systematic: the BFP texts vehicle status to the LGU after each maintenance event, CDRRMO personnel track availability through group chats and manual vehicle labeling, and CHO staff confirm vehicle readiness by sight since all three ambulances share the same compound. All four agencies maintain vehicle records, driver assignments, and license expiration dates in separate paper records that are not consolidated or searchable, and the PNP is the only agency with a digital tool for vehicle management, a Preventive Maintenance System (PMS) that covers only repair record logging, while the CDRRMO separately operates a data management software for emergency response analytics rather than fleet maintenance monitoring. An interview with the City GSO Motorpool further confirmed that damage descriptions submitted by agency officers are sometimes incomplete, as the Motorpool team regularly discovers additional defects during pre-inspection that were not included in the original report. In a setting where vehicle readiness directly affects emergency response outcomes, these documentation gaps and the absence of a centralized monitoring system across all four agencies create operational risk that manual records and informal communication cannot consistently address.  
To address these conditions, this study proposes the development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System (RVMS), a two-platform digital solution consisting of an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators. The mobile application allows drivers to view their assigned vehicle's details, submit digital BLOWBAGETS daily inspections, file damage reports with photo attachments, and receive notifications for preventive maintenance schedules and vehicle status changes. The administrative dashboard allows Agency Administrators to manage vehicle and driver records, monitor license expiry, review inspection results and damage reports, log and close dispatch activities, schedule and track preventive maintenance, log repair activities, and generate printable reports for inspections, damage reports, maintenance history, preventive maintenance, dispatch logs, and vehicle status. Each Agency Administrator's access is limited to their own agency's records, keeping vehicle data, inspection submissions, and dispatch logs separated by agency within the same system. The RVMS targets the CDRRMO, BFP, CHO, and PNP as participating agencies, with the aim of replacing their current paper forms, verbal reporting, and fragmented monitoring practices with a single digital platform through which vehicle data is consistently recorded and accessible to both drivers and administrators. 

### **Purpose and Description** 

The Rescue Vehicle Management System is a two-platform digital solution developed to centralize vehicle and driver record management, daily inspection monitoring, defect and damage reporting, repair logging, preventive maintenance scheduling, dispatch logging, and report generation for the CDRRMO, BFP, CHO, and PNP in Calbayog City. The system has two user-facing platforms: an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators, with each Agency Administrator's access limited to records belonging to their own agency. Through the mobile application, Authorized Drivers can view their assigned vehicle's details and current status, submit digital BLOWBAGETS inspections with per-item status entries and remarks for flagged items, file damage reports with descriptions and optional photo attachments, and receive in-app notifications for preventive maintenance schedules and vehicle status updates. Through the web dashboard, Agency Administrators can manage vehicle and driver records, monitor automated license expiry alerts, review submitted inspection results and damage reports, log repair activities, track preventive maintenance schedules, and record and close dispatch activities. A dashboard summary screen displays current counts for vehicle operational statuses, total drivers, expiring licenses, and pending damage reports, while the report generation module allows Agency Administrators to produce filtered, printable records for inspections, damage reports, repair and maintenance history, preventive maintenance, dispatch logs, and vehicle status. Vehicle operational status across both platforms uses four values: Operational, Dispatched, Not Operational, and Under Preventive Maintenance. Agency Administrators receive in-app notifications for newly submitted damage reports, approaching or expired driver licenses, and preventive maintenance schedules with a Due Soon or Due status.

**Objectives**

The main objective of this study is to develop a Rescue Vehicle Management System. Specifically;

1. To input vehicle and driver information.  
2. To track vehicle maintenance.  
3. To provide an admin module for dispatch and vehicle management.  
4. To generate relevant reports.  
   

**Scope and Limitations**  
The study covers the design and development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System for four government agencies in Calbayog City: the Bureau of Fire Protection, Philippine National Police, City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office, and City Health Office. The system consists of an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators, with each Agency Administrator's access limited to records belonging to their own agency. Vehicle and driver record management will store vehicle details including vehicle type, plate number, make, model, engine number, chassis number, current mileage, assigned agency, and assigned driver, alongside driver information covering name, agency, license number, and license expiry date, with automated monitoring for approaching and expired driver licenses. The Digital BLOWBAGETS Inspection module will allow drivers to submit daily vehicle inspections using twelve standard checklist items (Battery, Lights, Oil, Water, Brakes, Air, Gas, Engine, Tires, Power Steering, Horn/Siren, and Directional Signals), with two additional items (Hydraulic System and Fire Pump) applicable to BFP drivers; each item is assigned a status of OK or Has Issue, with remarks required for flagged items, and Agency Administrators can review inspection results, view inspection history per vehicle and per driver, and monitor frequently reported issues. The Damage Report Management module will allow drivers to submit standardized damage reports with the nature of damage, suspected defective parts, and optional photo attachments; each report carries a default status of Pending and is marked Reviewed by the Agency Administrator after assessment, which also includes updating the vehicle's operational status. Repair Logging will document repair activities with scope of work, parts replaced, optional cost, repair source (Internal Office, GSO Motorpool, or External Repair Shop), assigned driver, and remarks, with vehicle operational status updated separately by the Agency Administrator after the repair is logged. Preventive Maintenance Scheduling will support mileage-based and time-based intervals per vehicle, with a configurable Due Soon warning threshold set by the Agency Administrator, PM status values of Due Soon, Due, and Completed, and completion fields for date serviced, repair source, parts replaced, and remarks; PM schedules are not auto-renewed and must be manually created for each maintenance cycle. Dispatch Logging will allow Agency Administrators to record vehicle dispatches with vehicle, driver, mission type (Fire Response, Medical Response, Rescue Operation, Patrol, Administrative Travel, or Others), location, and date and time out; vehicle status changes automatically to Dispatched when a dispatch is opened, and Agency Administrators are prompted to set the return status (Operational, Not Operational, or Under Preventive Maintenance) when the dispatch is closed with date and time in. A dashboard summary will display current counts for Operational, Dispatched, Not Operational, and Under Preventive Maintenance vehicles, total drivers, expiring licenses, and pending damage reports. Report generation will cover inspection records, damage and defect reports, repair and maintenance history, preventive maintenance records, dispatch logs, and vehicle status summaries, each accessible with date range, vehicle, driver, and other applicable filters. In-app notifications for Authorized Drivers will cover preventive maintenance reminders and vehicle status updates, while notifications for Agency Administrators will cover newly submitted damage reports, approaching or expired driver licenses, and preventive maintenance schedules with a Due Soon or Due status.   
The proposed system will be limited to rescue vehicle operations and maintenance monitoring for the four participating agencies within Calbayog City, and will support two user roles only: Authorized Drivers and Agency Administrators. GSO personnel, external mechanics, and third-party repair providers will not have direct access to the system. Both platforms require a stable internet connection for data submission, synchronization, notifications, and dashboard access. The mobile application supports Android devices only and will not be available on iOS platforms. The system will not include GPS tracking, GIS mapping, route navigation, IoT-based vehicle diagnostics, telematics integration, or automated dispatch recommendation features. Vehicle inspections, damage reports, repair records, and preventive maintenance data depend on manual user input, and mileage-based preventive maintenance tracking relies on current mileage values manually updated by Agency Administrators in the vehicle records. The dispatch module supports only dispatch logging and fleet availability monitoring and does not include real-time vehicle tracking, emergency communication systems, or automated dispatch assignment; the assignment of vehicles and drivers to missions remains dependent on each participating agency's internal procedures. Although the system supports repair logging and vehicle status monitoring, actual repair validation, mechanical inspections, procurement, and external maintenance processes remain outside the system's scope. Vehicles under manufacturer warranty will be marked as Not Operational when damaged or under repair, with the actual repair process remaining dependent on authorized dealers or external service providers. Generated reports are based solely on encoded data and will not produce officially formatted government forms or legally standardized Commission on Audit documents. The study focuses on system development, workflow implementation, and pilot-level testing within the duration of the capstone project. 

**CHAPTER 2**

### **REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE/SYSTEMS**

This chapter presents the Review of Related Literature (RRL) and Review of Related Systems (RRS) relevant to the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System. The reviewed studies and existing systems discuss concepts, technologies, workflows, and practices related to vehicle and driver information management, dispatch availability monitoring, vehicle availability monitoring, preventive maintenance monitoring, administrative coordination, maintenance reporting, and centralized fleet management systems. The purpose of this chapter is to examine existing knowledge and technologies that may support the development of the proposed system, identify operational gaps and limitations in current practices, and establish the relevance of the study within the context of rescue vehicle maintenance and management among public safety agencies.    
Managing vehicle and driver records through centralized digital systems can reduce the inefficiencies and data risks associated with fragmented, paper-based processes. Vinay M. G. et al. (2025) discussed the OneStop Vehicle Information System, a platform designed to consolidate vehicle registration, driver licensing, and insurance verification into a centralized digital environment. The study emphasized that fragmented manual records often lead to delays, inconsistencies, and operational inefficiencies, while centralized databases with role-based access control provide a more secure and organized approach to information management. Similarly, Richard Gorham et al. (2025) examined a Motor Vehicle Information Management System framework that explored how vehicle and driver data could be stored and validated across multiple countries. Their findings highlighted that centralized databases using verified vehicle identification records and authenticated driver credentials improved record accuracy and strengthened long-term monitoring capabilities. Research conducted by A. M. Mimbala et al. (2024) also explored the use of a QR-code-based vehicle monitoring system intended to replace manual logbook recording practices in institutional settings. The system captured plate numbers, owner details, vehicle categories, and designation records through a centralized database. The researchers noted that manual recording methods frequently resulted in inaccurate entries, delayed reporting, and potential data loss. The reviewed studies consistently highlight the value of centralized information management in improving record accuracy, accessibility, and long-term monitoring. By replacing fragmented and paper-based records with a unified database, organizations can reduce administrative inefficiencies and improve data reliability. These concepts are relevant to the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System, particularly in supporting vehicle and driver record management, vehicle assignment tracking, and license expiry monitoring.     
Vehicle maintenance tracking systems have been implemented across various fleet environments to improve reliability and reduce the operational costs associated with unplanned repairs and maintenance delays. Alejandro Crespo del Castillo and Ajith Kumar Parlikad (2024) explained that dynamic fleet management systems integrating predictive maintenance, preventive scheduling, and workload balancing can minimize operational costs while maintaining fleet availability. Their findings revealed that monitoring asset conditions and adjusting maintenance schedules according to component criticality improved maintenance allocation and reduced the risks associated with unexpected failures. Likewise, G. A. Piña Silva et al. (2025) utilized a discrete event simulation model to analyze corrective maintenance workflows in a government-operated vehicle fleet, focusing on reducing repair delays and workshop bottlenecks. The researchers found that structured maintenance workflow monitoring significantly improved vehicle availability and reduced repair turnaround time. Similarly, Marife M. Gallega (2025) reported that effective vehicle maintenance management relied heavily on preventive maintenance scheduling, utilization monitoring, and organized maintenance planning. The study emphasized that systematic monitoring procedures contributed significantly to efficient institutional vehicle operations. Across the reviewed studies, maintenance activities were shown to be more effective when supported by structured monitoring and preventive servicing procedures. The findings suggest that timely inspections, organized maintenance records, and scheduled maintenance contribute to higher vehicle availability and operational readiness. These principles provide a foundation for the maintenance-related functions of the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System.    
Centralized administrative and dispatch management systems have been developed to improve operational coordination, vehicle monitoring, and fleet availability across organizations. B. R. Lubis and Suendri (2025) developed a web-based fleet management information system that integrated fleet records, maintenance scheduling, driver management, and operational monitoring within a centralized administrative dashboard. Their findings demonstrated that centralized systems improved operational efficiency, monitoring accuracy, and administrative coordination. Similarly, A. Khaliq et al. (2025) designed a web-based Vehicle Management Information System with role-based access control that managed vehicle records, maintenance scheduling, and operational monitoring through a centralized dashboard. The researchers observed that automating administrative processes improved data traceability and operational coordination. In addition, Carlos Bolaños et al. (2022) developed a Fleet Management and Control System that incorporated vehicle assignment, route monitoring, and enterprise-level operational control for transportation services. Their findings revealed that centralized dispatch and monitoring systems improved accountability, coordination, and real-time operational visibility among fleet administrators. A common theme among the reviewed studies is the importance of centralized administrative oversight in managing fleet operations. Integrating vehicle monitoring, driver management, and dispatch-related activities within a single platform improves coordination and provides administrators with better visibility of operational resources. Such findings reinforce the need for dispatch logging, fleet availability monitoring, and vehicle status management within the proposed system. The reviewed studies also emphasize the importance of maintaining accurate vehicle status information, as dispatch decisions depend heavily on knowing whether vehicles are available, deployed, under maintenance, or otherwise unavailable for operations.   
Digital reporting systems have been implemented across fleet and public service operations to reduce manual documentation workloads and improve the accessibility of operational records. Geraldine G. Nerona et al. (2025) developed digital management systems that automated the generation of operational and administrative reports within a local government environment. Their findings demonstrated that automated reporting significantly improved data management efficiency and reduced the burden of manually retrieving and consolidating records. Likewise, Gian Louis D. Intal et al. (2019) developed a computer-based maintenance system that incorporated automated report generation and visual analytics for monitoring maintenance operations. The researchers emphasized that integrating reporting directly into maintenance workflows improved operational monitoring and supported data-driven decision-making. Furthermore, P. B. Jha et al. (2023) explained that a web-based Vehicle Maintenance System incorporated printable and date-filtered reporting features to improve accessibility and organization of maintenance information. The study noted that centralized digital reporting reduced delays associated with manual documentation and improved the retrieval of operational records. The reviewed literature indicates that automated reporting systems improve record accessibility while reducing the effort required to retrieve and consolidate operational information. By generating reports directly from stored records, organizations can strengthen monitoring activities and support more informed administrative decisions. These capabilities are reflected in the reporting functions proposed for the Rescue Vehicle Management System.     
Fleetio is a cloud-based fleet management platform developed by Rarestep, Inc. in the United States, designed to centralize vehicle and driver records, automate preventive maintenance scheduling, manage work orders, and generate maintenance and inspection reports for both private and public sector fleets (Fleetio, n.d.). The platform operates through a web-based interface with companion mobile applications for iOS and Android, supporting functions related to vehicle and driver management, maintenance monitoring, dispatch coordination, and report generation. Despite its comprehensive feature set, users have reported that the mobile application provides limited functionality compared to the desktop version, particularly for advanced administrative tasks. The system also relies heavily on third-party telematics integrations for real-time vehicle tracking, which may introduce additional hardware costs and setup complexity for resource-constrained local government units. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these limitations by providing a unified mobile and web platform specifically designed for LGU rescue vehicle workflows without requiring external telematics hardware.  
Samsara, developed by Samsara Inc. in the United States, is an Internet of Things-based fleet management platform that combines GPS tracking, AI dashboard cameras, driver behavior monitoring, dispatch visibility, and maintenance alerts through a cloud-hosted dashboard and companion mobile applications (Samsara, 2025). The system has been widely adopted by municipal and government fleets and provides administrators with real-time visibility of vehicle locations, driver activity, and fleet-wide operational metrics relevant to dispatch monitoring and administrative oversight. However, its maintenance module functions mainly as an extension of its telematics capabilities rather than as a dedicated maintenance management system, leaving gaps in preventive maintenance scheduling depth and repair documentation. Its hardware-dependent architecture and subscription-based pricing also make it impractical for smaller LGUs with limited financial resources. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these limitations by incorporating an integrated maintenance tracking, dispatch logging, and vehicle status monitoring module that will operate without costly IoT hardware.  
Chevin FleetWave, developed by Chevin Fleet Solutions in the United Kingdom, is an enterprise-level fleet information system used by government and public sector organizations to manage vehicle assets, enforce driver compliance, schedule maintenance, and generate regulatory inspection reports (PCMag, 2018). The system is web-based and is particularly recognized for producing compliance and inspection documentation aligned with formal regulatory standards, making it a relevant reference for the report generation objective of the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System. Despite its strengths, the platform has a steep learning curve, and customizing report templates or data fields often requires significant effort or vendor assistance. This limits its adaptability for LGUs that require localized documentation such as printable defect reports, maintenance records, and operational status reports. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address this issue by offering simplified and localized reporting modules specifically designed for Philippine rescue vehicle operations.  
Verizon Connect, developed by Verizon in the United States, is a Software-as-a-Service fleet management platform that provides administrators with a centralized dashboard for monitoring vehicle locations, managing driver assignments, monitoring dispatch activities, and overseeing fleet operations across organizations of varying sizes (Verizon Connect, 2026). Its centralized command interface serves as a relevant reference for the administrative and dispatch monitoring functions of the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System by demonstrating how operational data and vehicle status information can be presented within a unified interface. However, the platform has been criticized for creating data silos where maintenance records do not integrate efficiently with other operational modules, leading to fragmented information that weakens informed decision-making. Its relatively slow innovation cycle has also been viewed as a limitation in responding to evolving organizational needs. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these concerns through a unified database structure where vehicle records, inspections, maintenance logs, dispatch records, and operational statuses will be managed as one interconnected system.  
Geotab, developed by Geotab Inc. in Canada, is an open-platform telematics solution that extracts detailed vehicle diagnostic data — including engine health, battery status, and fuel consumption — through its MyGeotab cloud dashboard (Geotab, 2026). The system provides administrators with extensive technical information that can guide maintenance scheduling decisions and fleet monitoring activities. However, users subscribed to Geotab's base plan are restricted from accessing fuel usage data and advanced engine-hour metrics, both of which are essential for mileage-based and time-based preventive maintenance scheduling. This tiered access model limits the ability of lower-budget agencies to fully utilize the platform for maintenance planning without additional subscription costs. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address this limitation by incorporating built-in preventive maintenance scheduling based on mileage thresholds and time intervals identified during the study's data collection phase without relying on subscription-based telematics services.  
TrackMe Suite, developed by Webcast Technologies Inc. in the Philippines, is a locally deployed GPS tracking and fleet management system used by major Philippine corporations and select government agencies to monitor fuel consumption, vehicle location, and driver behavior through web and mobile interfaces (Webcast Technologies Inc., 2026). As a Philippine-developed platform, it demonstrates the feasibility of implementing web-based fleet monitoring systems within the country's infrastructure context. However, the platform is primarily intended for logistics and cargo delivery operations and lacks preventive maintenance scheduling, repair documentation, inspection monitoring, and standardized reporting modules required by government agencies. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these limitations by integrating digital inspections, maintenance tracking, dispatch monitoring, and report generation modules specifically tailored for rescue vehicle operations within Philippine LGUs.  
The Pure Force Citizens App and Command Center, developed by Pure Force and Rescue Corp. in the Philippines, is an emergency response platform deployed across several LGUs and agencies including the PNP and BFP (Manila Bulletin, 2022). The system enables citizens to report emergencies while allowing dispatchers to locate and mobilize the nearest rescue units through a mobile application and command dashboard. The platform demonstrates that Philippine government agencies are capable of adopting digital tools for emergency response management and serves as a relevant reference for the dispatch logging and fleet availability monitoring functions of the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System. However, Pure Force operates primarily as an incident command and dispatch platform and does not provide modules for monitoring vehicle maintenance conditions such as inspection records, repair histories, or preventive maintenance schedules. As a result, agencies using the system cannot effectively determine whether rescue vehicles are mechanically ready before deployment. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address this gap by integrating dispatch monitoring with maintenance and vehicle operational status management within a single platform.  
PhilTrack, developed by Skyhawk Technology in the Philippines, is a web-based GPS tracking solution intended for small enterprises and personal vehicle owners (PhilTrack, n.d.). The system offers anti-theft monitoring, remote vehicle locking, and basic location tracking through a web interface. In relation to the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System, PhilTrack demonstrates the local demand for accessible web-based vehicle monitoring tools and supports the feasibility of implementing a web-based administrative platform for LGU vehicle operations. However, the system lacks comprehensive reporting functions, preventive maintenance scheduling, inspection management, and driver record monitoring necessary for rescue vehicle operations. It also does not support dispatch logging or operational vehicle status tracking. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these shortcomings by integrating vehicle and driver management, digital inspections, maintenance scheduling, dispatch monitoring, and standardized report generation within a centralized platform tailored for rescue fleet management.

The reviewed literature and existing systems consistently emphasize the importance of centralized vehicle information management, preventive maintenance monitoring, operational coordination, and automated reporting in improving fleet efficiency and vehicle readiness. Studies revealed that digital databases enhance the accuracy and accessibility of vehicle and driver records, while structured maintenance monitoring helps reduce unexpected breakdowns and improve vehicle availability. Likewise, research on administrative and dispatch management demonstrated that centralized monitoring and status tracking strengthen coordination and operational visibility. Existing fleet management systems such as Fleetio, Samsara, Verizon Connect, and Geotab further illustrate how technology can support vehicle monitoring, maintenance scheduling, dispatch coordination, and report generation through integrated digital platforms. Collectively, these studies and systems confirm that the use of centralized information systems contributes significantly to more organized, efficient, and data-driven fleet operations. 

Despite these advancements, several limitations remain evident. Many existing systems depend on costly telematics hardware, subscription-based services, or enterprise-level infrastructure that may not be practical for local government agencies. Other systems focus primarily on dispatching, GPS monitoring, or maintenance management without integrating these functions into a single platform. In addition, the reviewed systems do not adequately address the specific maintenance workflows, inspection procedures, reporting practices, and operational requirements observed among rescue vehicle agencies in Calbayog City. Based on the interview findings, participating agencies already implement vehicle inspections, defect reporting, maintenance procedures, dispatch logging, and vehicle monitoring practices. However, these activities are often managed through separate logbooks, paper forms, spreadsheets, radio communications, and manual coordination among different personnel. As a result, vehicle information, maintenance records, dispatch activities, and operational status updates are fragmented across multiple records and offices. This situation makes it difficult to obtain a centralized view of vehicle readiness and fleet availability. These gaps highlight the need for a localized Rescue Vehicle Management System that integrates vehicle and driver management, digital inspections, damage reporting, maintenance tracking, preventive maintenance scheduling, dispatch logging, fleet availability monitoring, report generation, and notification functions within a single centralized platform tailored to the operational requirements of rescue agencies in Calbayog City.

These human resources are essential to ensure the effective operation and sustainability of the Rescue Vehicle Management System during pilot testing and actual deployment.

The Planning and Data Gathering phase, covering May, will focus on initial system planning, background research, review of related literature and existing operational procedures, identification of system scope, and workflow analysis. This phase will also include the drafting of Chapters 1 to 3, which will cover the introduction of the problem, analysis of existing maintenance and dispatch procedures across agencies, and definition of technical requirements. In addition, structured interviews and data gathering will be conducted with key stakeholders from the Philippine National Police, Bureau of Fire Protection, City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office, and City Health Office to validate operational workflows, identify common maintenance and dispatch practices, and refine the proposed system features based on actual agency operations.

The Proposed Prototype Design phase, covering June, will focus on UI/UX prototype design and the finalization of system workflows based on the data gathered from the participating agencies. This phase will involve the design of interface mockups for both the Android mobile application and the web-based administrative dashboard, ensuring that the proposed system layout and navigation align with the operational needs of Authorized Drivers and Agency Administrators.  

The Software Development phase, spanning July to September, will cover the development and integration of the proposed system, including the web-based administrative dashboard, Android mobile application for drivers, centralized database integration, digital inspection module, defect and damage reporting module, repair logging module, preventive maintenance scheduling, dispatch logging and fleet monitoring module, notification system, and report generation functionalities. This phase will also include continuous system refinement and functionality testing during development. 

Finally, the Testing, and Deployment phase, covering October to November, will include unit testing, integration testing, user acceptance testing, bug fixing, pilot testing, deployment preparation, and finalization of documentation including Chapters 4 and 5\. This phase will also include preparation for the final presentation and system defense.
