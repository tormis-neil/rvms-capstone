**CHAPTER 1**

## **INTRODUCTION**

### **Project Context** 

 Across different countries, emergency and government vehicle fleets are still managed through manual or fragmented recording systems that create gaps in data accuracy and vehicle readiness. Vinay M. G. et al. (2025) emphasized that manual and disconnected vehicle information processes often result in inconsistent records, delayed verification, and poor information accessibility. Similarly, Richard Gorham et al. (2025) highlighted that the lack of centralized vehicle and driver databases weakens long-term monitoring and record validation across agencies. Crespo del Castillo and Parlikad (2024) further explained that inadequate maintenance scheduling and monitoring increase the risks of unexpected vehicle failures and operational downtime. In addition, Carlos Bolaños et al. (2022) found that the absence of centralized dispatch and fleet coordination systems contributes to poor operational visibility, scheduling irregularities, and reduced accountability among fleet operators. These studies collectively showed that many fleet environments still struggle with inefficient vehicle monitoring, maintenance coordination, dispatch management, and administrative reporting processes, particularly when operations rely heavily on manual procedures and fragmented systems.   
Philippine-based studies also identified several operational challenges related to vehicle maintenance monitoring, documentation, and administrative coordination within institutional and government environments. A. M. Mimbala et al. (2024) observed that manual logbook-based vehicle monitoring practices often lead to inaccurate records, delayed reporting, and data loss. Likewise, Marife M. Gallega (2025) emphasized that effective maintenance management depends heavily on organized preventive maintenance scheduling and systematic monitoring procedures to sustain vehicle reliability. Geraldine G. Nerona et al. (2025) also reported that manual administrative reporting increases the difficulty of retrieving, consolidating, and managing operational records within local government operations. Furthermore, Gian Louis D. Intal et al. (2019) noted that maintenance monitoring and reporting processes become inefficient when organizations rely on manual documentation and disconnected workflows. These findings indicate that many Philippine institutions and agencies continue to face challenges in vehicle maintenance coordination, operational monitoring, dispatch visibility, and report generation due to the continued reliance on paper-based and fragmented administrative processes.  
The four government agencies managing rescue vehicles in Calbayog City — the City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office (CDRRMO), the Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP), the City Health Office (CHO), and the Philippine National Police (PNP) — collectively operate a fleet of rescue vehicles including ambulances, fire trucks, and patrol cars that serve the city's emergency response needs across its barangays. Interviews conducted with officers from each agency confirmed that vehicle management practices across all four rely on manual, paper-based processes with no shared centralized digital system in place. The BFP documents daily vehicle inspections using a paper-based Motor Vehicle Daily Safety Checklist following the BLOWBAGETS protocol and records defect and maintenance history in a physical Motor Vehicle Ledger Card, while the CDRRMO and CHO depend on informal, driver-triggered defect reporting with no standardized daily inspection form, and the PNP communicates all maintenance concerns verbally with no formal documentation forms at the station level. Regarding vehicle availability, the BFP reports vehicle status as "Serviceable" or "Unserviceable" to the BFP Provincial Office and to the LGU through text after every maintenance action, while the CDRRMO manually labels vehicles "Not Operational" when grounded for repair — both practices reflecting a shared operational need across all four agencies to communicate vehicle deployment readiness to command and oversight offices, yet without a centralized system through which availability can be checked at any given time. All four agencies separately track vehicle plate numbers, driver assignments, and driver license records in individual paper records that are not consolidated in any shared or searchable system, and among the four, the PNP Calbayog was found to be the only agency with an existing digital tool — a software called the Preventive Maintenance System (PMS) — though it is limited to repair record logging and does not cover inspection workflows, dispatch monitoring, or standardized report generation.  
The reliance on manual recording, verbal coordination, and fragmented paper trails across these four agencies creates compounding operational gaps that individual effort alone cannot consistently close. Paper-based inspection forms and informal defect reporting create conditions where vehicle condition details are lost or inconsistently recorded, reducing the reliability of maintenance decisions made by agency officers. Without a centralized platform for monitoring vehicle availability, agency personnel have no reliable way to determine at any given time whether a rescue vehicle is operational, currently dispatched on a mission, or grounded for repair — a visibility gap that becomes critical in emergency response settings where vehicle readiness directly affects public safety outcomes. Preventive maintenance monitoring is handled differently across agencies, with some following mileage or time-based standards and others relying solely on driver observation, increasing the risk of missed servicing when records are not consistently updated. Vehicle assignments, plate numbers, and driver license records are maintained separately by each agency with no mechanism for generating consolidated reports or flagging compliance deadlines in advance. These conditions collectively justify the development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System — a two-platform digital solution consisting of an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators — designed to digitize vehicle and driver records, standardize daily inspection and defect reporting, monitor vehicle dispatch availability, enforce preventive maintenance schedules, log repair actions, and generate vehicle and maintenance reports aligned with the operational needs of the participating agencies. 

### **Purpose and Description** 

The Rescue Vehicle Management System is a two-platform digital system developed to address the manual, fragmented vehicle management practices of the four rescue agencies operating in Calbayog City: the CDRRMO, BFP, CHO, and PNP. The system centralizes vehicle and driver records, daily inspection monitoring, defect and damage reporting, repair logging, preventive maintenance scheduling, dispatch logging, and report generation within a shared digital platform — replacing the paper-based forms, verbal reporting, and disconnected agency-level practices confirmed across all four agencies during data gathering. The system is designed to preserve the existing operational structures and chain-of-command procedures of each participating agency while providing a centralized environment through which vehicle-related information and maintenance activities are organized, monitored, and documented.

The system consists of two platforms: an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators. Through the mobile application, Authorized Drivers can perform and submit digital BLOWBAGETS inspections for their assigned vehicles, submit defect and damage reports with optional photo attachments, view their vehicle's current status and maintenance history, and receive notifications for inspection confirmations, repair completions, dispatch logging, and upcoming preventive maintenance schedules. Through the web dashboard, Agency Administrators can manage vehicle and driver records, monitor driver license expiration, review submitted inspection results and defect reports, update vehicle operational status, log and close dispatch records, schedule and track preventive maintenance, log repair actions, and generate printable reports covering inspections, defects, repairs, preventive maintenance, dispatch history, and vehicle operational status. A fleet availability board on the dashboard provides a view of all vehicles and their current status — Operational, Dispatched, Partially Operational, Not Operational, or Under Preventive Maintenance — allowing Agency Administrators to determine vehicle availability at any given time. In-app notifications are delivered within the mobile application for Authorized Drivers and within the web dashboard for Agency Administrators, keeping both roles informed of relevant updates without requiring separate communication channels.

**Scope and Limitations**  
The study will focus on the design and development of Rescue Vehicle Management System for selected government agencies in Calbayog City, including the Bureau of Fire Protection, Philippine National Police, City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office, and the City Health Office. The proposed system will consist of a mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based dashboard for Agency Admins. The system will include vehicle and driver record management capable of storing vehicle details, driver information, license records, assigned vehicles, and automated license expiry monitoring. It will also include a Digital BLOWBAGETS Inspection module where drivers can conduct daily inspections, submit inspection results with remarks, and allow Agency Admins to review inspection history and update vehicle operational status. A Defect and Damage Reporting module will enable drivers to submit standardized damage reports with descriptions and optional photos, while Agency Admins can assess reports, update vehicle statuses, and generate printable reports for external coordination. The system will also include repair logging functions for documenting repair activities, replaced parts, repair providers, repair dates, and printable repair logs. Preventive Maintenance Scheduling and Tracking will support mileage-based and time-based maintenance intervals, preventive maintenance notifications, maintenance status monitoring, and logging of completed servicing activities. In addition, the system will include Dispatch Logging and Fleet Availability Monitoring features that allow Agency Admins to record dispatch activities, assign vehicles and drivers, monitor mission information, and view the operational availability of rescue vehicles through a centralized dashboard. Vehicle operational statuses supported by the system will include Operational, Dispatched, Partially Operational, Not Operational, and Under Preventive Maintenance. The system will further support report generation for inspections, defect reports, maintenance history, preventive maintenance records, dispatch logs, and vehicle operational statuses. Lastly, a notification system will provide alerts and updates related to inspections, defect reports, preventive maintenance schedules, dispatch logging, repair completion, vehicle status changes, and driver license expiration monitoring.   
The proposed system will be limited to rescue vehicle operations and maintenance monitoring for the selected participating agencies within Calbayog City. The study will only support two primary user roles: Authorized Drivers and Agency Admins. The system will not include direct access for GSO personnel, external mechanics, or third-party repair providers. Both the mobile application and the web-based dashboard will require a stable internet connection for data submission, synchronization, notifications, and dashboard access. The mobile application will only support Android devices and will not support iOS platforms. The system will not include GPS tracking, GIS mapping, route navigation, IoT-based vehicle diagnostics, telematics integration, or automated dispatch recommendation features. Vehicle inspections, mileage monitoring, defect reporting, repair encoding, and preventive maintenance records will depend on manual user input. The dispatch module will only support dispatch logging and fleet availability monitoring and will not include dispatch assignment features, real-time emergency communication systems, or live tracking of deployed vehicles. The assignment of vehicles and drivers to missions will remain dependent on the existing internal procedures of each participating agency. Although the system will support repair logging and vehicle status monitoring, actual repair validation, mechanical inspections, procurement procedures, and external maintenance processes will remain outside the scope of the system. Newly acquired vehicles under manufacturer warranty will still be marked as “Not Operational” when damaged or undergoing repair; however, the actual repair process will remain dependent on authorized dealers or external service providers outside the system. Generated reports will only rely on encoded records and will not automatically produce official government forms or legally standardized COA documents. The study will primarily focus on system development, workflow implementation, and pilot-level testing within the duration of the capstone project.

### **REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE/SYSTEMS**

This chapter presents the Review of Related Literature (RRL) and Review of Related Systems (RRS) relevant to the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System. The reviewed studies and existing systems discuss concepts, technologies, workflows, and practices related to vehicle and driver information management, dispatch availability monitoring, preventive maintenance monitoring, administrative coordination, maintenance reporting, and centralized fleet management systems. The purpose of this chapter is to examine existing knowledge and technologies that may support the development of the proposed system, identify operational gaps and limitations in current practices, and establish the relevance of the study within the context of rescue vehicle maintenance and management among public safety agencies.    
Managing vehicle and driver records through centralized digital systems can reduce the inefficiencies and data risks associated with fragmented, paper-based processes. Vinay M. G. et al. (2025) discussed the OneStop Vehicle Information System, a platform designed to consolidate vehicle registration, driver licensing, and insurance verification into a centralized digital environment. The study emphasized that fragmented manual records often lead to delays, inconsistencies, and operational inefficiencies, while centralized databases with role-based access control provide a more secure and organized approach to information management. Similarly, Richard Gorham et al. (2025) examined a Motor Vehicle Information Management System framework that explored how vehicle and driver data could be stored and validated across multiple countries. Their findings highlighted that centralized databases using verified vehicle identification records and authenticated driver credentials improved record accuracy and strengthened long-term monitoring capabilities. Research conducted by A. M. Mimbala et al. (2024) also explored the use of a QR-code-based vehicle monitoring system intended to replace manual logbook recording practices in institutional settings. The system captured plate numbers, owner details, vehicle categories, and designation records through a centralized database. The researchers noted that manual recording methods frequently resulted in inaccurate entries, delayed reporting, and potential data loss. Collectively, these studies demonstrate that centralized digital databases improve the reliability, accessibility, and consistency of vehicle and driver information. These findings support the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System, which will incorporate centralized vehicle and driver record management, vehicle assignment tracking, and license expiry monitoring for rescue vehicle operations.    
Vehicle maintenance tracking systems have been implemented across various fleet environments to improve reliability and reduce the operational costs associated with unplanned repairs and maintenance delays. Alejandro Crespo del Castillo and Ajith Kumar Parlikad (2024) explained that dynamic fleet management systems integrating predictive maintenance, preventive scheduling, and workload balancing can minimize operational costs while maintaining fleet availability. Their findings revealed that monitoring asset conditions and adjusting maintenance schedules according to component criticality improved maintenance allocation and reduced the risks associated with unexpected failures. Likewise, G. A. Piña Silva et al. (2025) utilized a discrete event simulation model to analyze corrective maintenance workflows in a government-operated vehicle fleet, focusing on reducing repair delays and workshop bottlenecks. The researchers found that structured maintenance workflow monitoring significantly improved vehicle availability and reduced repair turnaround time. Similarly, Marife M. Gallega (2025) reported that effective vehicle maintenance management relied heavily on preventive maintenance scheduling, utilization monitoring, and organized maintenance planning. The study emphasized that systematic monitoring procedures contributed significantly to efficient institutional vehicle operations. These findings support the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System, which will incorporate digital BLOWBAGETS inspection, defect and damage reporting, repair logging, preventive maintenance scheduling, and maintenance status monitoring to improve the operational readiness of rescue vehicles.   
Centralized administrative and dispatch management systems have been developed to improve operational coordination, vehicle monitoring, and fleet availability across organizations. B. R. Lubis and Suendri (2025) developed a web-based fleet management information system that integrated fleet records, maintenance scheduling, driver management, and operational monitoring within a centralized administrative dashboard. Their findings demonstrated that centralized systems improved operational efficiency, monitoring accuracy, and administrative coordination. Similarly, A. Khaliq et al. (2025) designed a web-based Vehicle Management Information System with role-based access control that managed vehicle records, maintenance scheduling, and operational monitoring through a centralized dashboard. The researchers observed that automating administrative processes improved data traceability and operational coordination. In addition, Carlos Bolaños et al. (2022) developed a Fleet Management and Control System that incorporated vehicle assignment, route monitoring, and enterprise-level operational control for transportation services. Their findings revealed that centralized dispatch and monitoring systems improved accountability, coordination, and real-time operational visibility among fleet administrators. These studies support the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System, which will incorporate dispatch logging, fleet availability monitoring, vehicle status management, and centralized administrative functions to support rescue vehicle coordination across participating agencies.  
Digital reporting systems have been implemented across fleet and public service operations to reduce manual documentation workloads and improve the accessibility of operational records. Geraldine G. Nerona et al. (2025) developed digital management systems that automated the generation of operational and administrative reports within a local government environment. Their findings demonstrated that automated reporting significantly improved data management efficiency and reduced the burden of manually retrieving and consolidating records. Likewise, Gian Louis D. The researchers emphasized that integrating reporting directly into maintenance workflows improved operational monitoring and supported data-driven decision-making. Furthermore, P. B. Jha et al. Taken together, these studies demonstrate that automated report generation improves operational monitoring, record accessibility, and administrative efficiency. These findings support the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System, which will generate inspection reports, maintenance histories, dispatch logs, preventive maintenance records, and vehicle operational status reports for rescue vehicle administrators.    
Fleetio is a cloud-based fleet management platform developed by Rarestep, Inc. in the United States, designed to centralize vehicle and driver records, automate preventive maintenance scheduling, manage work orders, and generate maintenance and inspection reports for both private and public sector fleets (Fleetio, n.d.). The platform operates through a web-based interface with companion mobile applications for iOS and Android, supporting functions related to vehicle and driver management, maintenance monitoring, dispatch coordination, and report generation. Despite its comprehensive feature set, users have reported that the mobile application provides limited functionality compared to the desktop version, particularly for advanced administrative tasks. The system also relies heavily on third-party telematics integrations for real-time vehicle tracking, which may introduce additional hardware costs and setup complexity for resource-constrained local government units. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these limitations by providing a unified mobile and web platform specifically designed for LGU rescue vehicle workflows without requiring external telematics hardware.  
Samsara, developed by Samsara Inc. in the United States, is an Internet of Things-based fleet management platform that combines GPS tracking, AI dashboard cameras, driver behavior monitoring, dispatch visibility, and maintenance alerts through a cloud-hosted dashboard and companion mobile applications (Samsara, 2025). The system has been widely adopted by municipal and government fleets and provides administrators with real-time visibility of vehicle locations, driver activity, and fleet-wide operational metrics relevant to dispatch monitoring and administrative oversight. However, its maintenance module functions mainly as an extension of its telematics capabilities rather than as a dedicated maintenance management system, leaving gaps in preventive maintenance scheduling depth and repair documentation. Its hardware-dependent architecture and subscription-based pricing also make it impractical for smaller LGUs with limited financial resources. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these limitations by incorporating an integrated maintenance tracking, dispatch logging, and vehicle status monitoring module that will operate without costly IoT hardware.  
Chevin FleetWave, developed by Chevin Fleet Solutions in the United Kingdom, is an enterprise-level fleet information system used by government and public sector organizations to manage vehicle assets, enforce driver compliance, schedule maintenance, and generate regulatory inspection reports (PCMag, 2018). The system is web-based and is particularly recognized for producing compliance and inspection documentation aligned with formal regulatory standards, making it a relevant reference for the report generation objective of the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System. Despite its strengths, the platform has a steep learning curve, and customizing report templates or data fields often requires significant effort or vendor assistance. This limits its adaptability for LGUs that require localized documentation such as printable defect reports, maintenance records, and operational status reports. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address this issue by offering simplified and localized reporting modules specifically designed for Philippine rescue vehicle operations.  
Verizon Connect, developed by Verizon in the United States, is a Software-as-a-Service fleet management platform that provides administrators with a centralized dashboard for monitoring vehicle locations, managing driver assignments, monitoring dispatch activities, and overseeing fleet operations across organizations of varying sizes (Verizon Connect, 2026). Its centralized command interface serves as a relevant reference for the administrative and dispatch monitoring functions of the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System by demonstrating how operational data and vehicle status information can be presented within a unified interface. However, the platform has been criticized for creating data silos where maintenance records do not integrate efficiently with other operational modules, leading to fragmented information that weakens informed decision-making. Its relatively slow innovation cycle has also been viewed as a limitation in responding to evolving organizational needs. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these concerns through a unified database structure where vehicle records, inspections, maintenance logs, dispatch records, and operational statuses will be managed as one interconnected system.  
Geotab, developed by Geotab Inc. in Canada, is an open-platform telematics solution that extracts detailed vehicle diagnostic data — including engine health, battery status, and fuel consumption — through its MyGeotab cloud dashboard (Geotab, 2026). The system provides administrators with extensive technical information that can guide maintenance scheduling decisions and fleet monitoring activities. However, users subscribed to Geotab's base plan are restricted from accessing fuel usage data and advanced engine-hour metrics, both of which are essential for mileage-based and time-based preventive maintenance scheduling. This tiered access model limits the ability of lower-budget agencies to fully utilize the platform for maintenance planning without additional subscription costs. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address this limitation by incorporating built-in preventive maintenance scheduling based on mileage thresholds and time intervals identified during the study's data collection phase without relying on subscription-based telematics services.  
TrackMe Suite, developed by Webcast Technologies Inc. in the Philippines, is a locally deployed GPS tracking and fleet management system used by major Philippine corporations and select government agencies to monitor fuel consumption, vehicle location, and driver behavior through web and mobile interfaces (Webcast Technologies Inc., 2026). As a Philippine-developed platform, it demonstrates the feasibility of implementing web-based fleet monitoring systems within the country's infrastructure context. However, the platform is primarily intended for logistics and cargo delivery operations and lacks preventive maintenance scheduling, repair documentation, inspection monitoring, and standardized reporting modules required by government agencies. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these limitations by integrating digital inspections, maintenance tracking, dispatch monitoring, and report generation modules specifically tailored for rescue vehicle operations within Philippine LGUs.  
The Pure Force Citizens App and Command Center, developed by Pure Force and Rescue Corp. in the Philippines, is an emergency response platform deployed across several LGUs and agencies including the PNP and BFP (Manila Bulletin, 2022). The system enables citizens to report emergencies while allowing dispatchers to locate and mobilize the nearest rescue units through a mobile application and command dashboard. The platform demonstrates that Philippine government agencies are capable of adopting digital tools for emergency response management and serves as a relevant reference for the dispatch logging and fleet availability monitoring functions of the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System. However, Pure Force operates primarily as an incident command and dispatch platform and does not provide modules for monitoring vehicle maintenance conditions such as inspection records, repair histories, or preventive maintenance schedules. As a result, agencies using the system cannot effectively determine whether rescue vehicles are mechanically ready before deployment. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address this gap by integrating dispatch monitoring with maintenance and vehicle operational status management within a single platform.  
PhilTrack, developed by Skyhawk Technology in the Philippines, is a web-based GPS tracking solution intended for small enterprises and personal vehicle owners (PhilTrack, n.d.). The system offers anti-theft monitoring, remote vehicle locking, and basic location tracking through a web interface. In relation to the proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System, PhilTrack demonstrates the local demand for accessible web-based vehicle monitoring tools and supports the feasibility of implementing a web-based administrative platform for LGU vehicle operations. However, the system lacks comprehensive reporting functions, preventive maintenance scheduling, inspection management, and driver record monitoring necessary for rescue vehicle operations. It also does not support dispatch logging or operational vehicle status tracking. The proposed Rescue Vehicle Management System will address these shortcomings by integrating vehicle and driver management, digital inspections, maintenance scheduling, dispatch monitoring, and standardized report generation within a centralized platform tailored for rescue fleet management.

**Figure 1\.** **Schedule of Activities for the Development and Implementation of Rescue Vehicle Management System**

The Planning and Data Gathering phase, covering May, will focus on initial system planning, background research, review of related literature and existing operational procedures, identification of system scope, and workflow analysis. This phase will also include the drafting of Chapters 1 to 3, which will cover the introduction of the problem, analysis of existing maintenance and dispatch procedures across agencies, and definition of technical requirements. In addition, structured interviews and data gathering will be conducted with key stakeholders from the Philippine National Police, Bureau of Fire Protection, City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office, and City Health Office to validate operational workflows, identify common maintenance and dispatch practices, and refine the proposed system features based on actual agency operations.

The Proposed Prototype Design phase, covering June, will focus on UI/UX prototype design and the finalization of system workflows based on the data gathered from the participating agencies. This phase will involve the design of interface mockups for both the Android mobile application and the web-based administrative dashboard, ensuring that the proposed system layout and navigation align with the operational needs of Authorized Drivers and Agency Administrators.  

The Software Development phase, spanning July to September, will cover the development and integration of the proposed system, including the web-based administrative dashboard, Android mobile application for drivers, centralized database integration, digital inspection module, defect and damage reporting module, repair logging module, preventive maintenance scheduling, dispatch logging and fleet monitoring module, notification system, and report generation functionalities. This phase will also include continuous system refinement and functionality testing during development. 

Finally, the Testing, and Deployment phase, covering October to November, will include unit testing, integration testing, user acceptance testing, bug fixing, pilot testing, deployment preparation, and finalization of documentation including Chapters 4 and 5\. This phase will also include preparation for the final presentation and system defense.
