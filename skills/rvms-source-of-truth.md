## CHAPTER 1 — INTRODUCTION

### Project Context

Government and emergency vehicle fleets in many countries still rely on manual recording systems and disconnected documentation practices that create recurring gaps in vehicle readiness and maintenance accountability. Vinay M. G. et al. (2025) found that manual vehicle information processes produce inconsistent records and poor data accessibility, while Richard Gorham et al. (2025) noted that the absence of centralized vehicle and driver databases weakens long-term monitoring and record validation. Crespo del Castillo and Parlikad (2024) determined that inadequate maintenance scheduling increases the risk of unexpected vehicle failures and operational downtime, and Carlos Bolaños et al. (2022) reported that fleet environments without centralized dispatch coordination suffer from poor operational visibility and reduced accountability. Philippine-based research shows the same problems. Mimbala et al. (2024) observed that manual logbook-based vehicle monitoring in institutional settings leads to inaccurate records and data loss, and Gallega (2025) noted that organized preventive maintenance scheduling is necessary for sustaining vehicle reliability. Nerona et al. (2025) found that manual administrative reporting increases the difficulty of consolidating and retrieving operational records within local government, while Intal et al. (2019) noted that maintenance monitoring deteriorates when agencies rely on paper-based and disconnected workflows.

In Calbayog City, four government agencies (the CDRRMO, BFP, CHO, and PNP) collectively operate ambulances, fire trucks, patrol cars, and other rescue vehicles deployed for emergency response across the city's barangays. Interviews conducted with officers from each agency confirmed that all four rely on manual, paper-based practices with no shared centralized system in place. The BFP documents daily vehicle inspections using a paper-based Motor Vehicle Daily Safety Checklist following the BLOWBAGETS protocol and records defect and maintenance history in a physical Motor Vehicle Ledger Card, while the CDRRMO and CHO depend on informal, driver-triggered defect reporting with no standardized daily inspection form, and the PNP communicates maintenance concerns verbally with no formal documentation at the station level. Fleet availability monitoring is no more systematic: the BFP texts vehicle status to the LGU after each maintenance event, CDRRMO personnel track availability through group chats and manual vehicle labeling, and CHO staff confirm vehicle readiness by sight since all three ambulances share the same compound. All four agencies maintain vehicle records, driver assignments, and license expiration dates in separate paper records that are not consolidated or searchable, and the PNP is the only agency with a digital tool for vehicle management, a Preventive Maintenance System (PMS) that covers only repair record logging, while the CDRRMO separately operates a data management software for emergency response analytics rather than fleet maintenance monitoring. An interview with the City GSO Motorpool further confirmed that damage descriptions submitted by agency officers are sometimes incomplete, as the Motorpool team regularly discovers additional defects during pre-inspection that were not included in the original report. In a setting where vehicle readiness directly affects emergency response outcomes, these documentation gaps and the absence of a centralized monitoring system across all four agencies create operational risk that manual records and informal communication cannot consistently address.

To address these conditions, this study proposes the development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System (RVMS), a two-platform digital solution consisting of an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators. The mobile application allows drivers to view their assigned vehicle's details, submit digital BLOWBAGETS daily inspections, file damage reports with photo attachments, and receive notifications for preventive maintenance schedules and vehicle status changes. The administrative dashboard allows Agency Administrators to manage vehicle and driver records, monitor license expiry, review inspection results and damage reports, log and close dispatch activities, schedule and track preventive maintenance, log repair activities, and generate printable reports for inspections, damage reports, maintenance history, preventive maintenance, dispatch logs, and vehicle status. Each Agency Administrator's access is limited to their own agency's records, keeping vehicle data, inspection submissions, and dispatch logs separated by agency within the same system. The RVMS targets the CDRRMO, BFP, CHO, and PNP as participating agencies, with the aim of replacing their current paper forms, verbal reporting, and fragmented monitoring practices with a single digital platform through which vehicle data is consistently recorded and accessible to both drivers and administrators.

### Purpose and Description

The Rescue Vehicle Management System is a two-platform digital solution developed to centralize vehicle and driver record management, daily inspection monitoring, defect and damage reporting, repair logging, preventive maintenance scheduling, dispatch logging, and report generation for the CDRRMO, BFP, CHO, and PNP in Calbayog City. The system has two user-facing platforms: an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators, with each Agency Administrator's access limited to records belonging to their own agency. Through the mobile application, Authorized Drivers can view their assigned vehicle's details and current status, submit digital BLOWBAGETS inspections with per-item status entries and remarks for flagged items, file damage reports with descriptions and optional photo attachments, and receive in-app notifications for preventive maintenance schedules and vehicle status updates. Through the web dashboard, Agency Administrators can manage vehicle and driver records, monitor automated license expiry alerts, review submitted inspection results and damage reports, log repair activities, track preventive maintenance schedules, and record and close dispatch activities. A dashboard summary screen displays current counts for vehicle operational statuses, total drivers, total vehicles, expiring licenses, and pending damage reports, while the report generation module allows Agency Administrators to produce filtered, printable records for inspections, damage reports, repair and maintenance history, preventive maintenance, dispatch logs, and vehicle status. Vehicle operational status across both platforms uses four values: Operational, Dispatched, Not Operational, and Under Preventive Maintenance. Agency Administrators receive in-app notifications for newly submitted damage reports, approaching or expired driver licenses, and preventive maintenance schedules with a Due Soon or Due status.

### Scope and Limitations

The study covers the design and development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System for four government agencies in Calbayog City: the Bureau of Fire Protection, Philippine National Police, City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office, and City Health Office. The system consists of an Android mobile application for Authorized Drivers and a web-based administrative dashboard for Agency Administrators, with each Agency Administrator's access limited to records belonging to their own agency. Vehicle and driver record management will store vehicle details including vehicle type, plate number, make, model, engine number, chassis number, current mileage, assigned agency, and assigned driver, alongside driver information covering name, agency, license number, and license expiry date, with automated monitoring for approaching and expired driver licenses. The Digital BLOWBAGETS Inspection module will allow drivers to submit daily vehicle inspections using twelve standard checklist items (Battery, Lights, Oil, Water, Brakes, Air, Gas, Engine, Tires, Power Steering, Horn/Siren, and Directional Signals), with two additional items (Hydraulic System and Fire Pump) applicable to BFP drivers; each item is assigned a status of OK or Has Issue, with remarks required for flagged items, and Agency Administrators can review inspection results, view inspection history per vehicle and per driver, and monitor frequently reported issues. The Damage Report Management module will allow drivers to submit standardized damage reports with the nature of damage, suspected defective parts, and optional photo attachments; each report carries a default status of Pending and is marked Reviewed by the Agency Administrator after assessment, which also includes updating the vehicle's operational status. Repair Logging will document repair activities with scope of work, parts replaced, optional cost, repair source (Internal Office, GSO Motorpool, or External Repair Shop), assigned driver, and remarks, with vehicle operational status updated separately by the Agency Administrator after the repair is logged. Preventive Maintenance Scheduling will support mileage-based and time-based intervals per vehicle, with a configurable Due Soon warning threshold set by the Agency Administrator, PM status values of Upcoming, Due Soon, Due, and Completed, and completion fields for date serviced, repair source, parts replaced, and remarks; PM schedules are not auto-renewed and must be manually created for each maintenance cycle. Dispatch Logging will allow Agency Administrators to record vehicle dispatches with vehicle, driver, mission type (Fire Response, Medical Response, Rescue Operation, Patrol, Administrative Travel, or Others), location, and date and time out; vehicle status changes automatically to Dispatched when a dispatch is opened, and Agency Administrators are prompted to set the return status (Operational, Not Operational, or Under Preventive Maintenance) when the dispatch is closed with date and time in. A dashboard summary will display current counts for Operational, Dispatched, Not Operational, and Under Preventive Maintenance vehicles, total drivers, total vehicles, expiring licenses, and pending damage reports. Report generation will cover inspection records, damage and defect reports, repair and maintenance history, preventive maintenance records, dispatch logs, and vehicle status summaries, each accessible with date range, vehicle, driver, and other applicable filters. In-app notifications for Authorized Drivers will cover preventive maintenance reminders and vehicle status updates, while notifications for Agency Administrators will cover newly submitted damage reports, approaching or expired driver licenses, and preventive maintenance schedules with a Due Soon or Due status.

The proposed system will be limited to rescue vehicle operations and maintenance monitoring for the four participating agencies within Calbayog City, and will support two user roles only: Authorized Drivers and Agency Administrators. GSO personnel, external mechanics, and third-party repair providers will not have direct access to the system. Agency Administrator accounts are provisioned within the system; only Authorized Drivers may self-register, and a self-registered driver account remains pending until an Agency Administrator of that agency approves or rejects it. Both platforms require a stable internet connection for data submission, synchronization, notifications, and dashboard access. The mobile application supports Android devices only and will not be available on iOS platforms. The system will not include GPS tracking, GIS mapping, route navigation, IoT-based vehicle diagnostics, telematics integration, or automated dispatch recommendation features. Vehicle inspections, damage reports, repair records, and preventive maintenance data depend on manual user input, and mileage-based preventive maintenance tracking relies on current mileage values manually updated by Agency Administrators in the vehicle records. The dispatch module supports only dispatch logging and fleet availability monitoring and does not include real-time vehicle tracking, emergency communication systems, or automated dispatch assignment; the assignment of vehicles and drivers to missions remains dependent on each participating agency's internal procedures. The system assigns one primary driver per vehicle record; rotating or shift-based driver arrangements, as practiced by CHO, are not accommodated within the current scope, and driver attribution in those cases relies on the dispatch record rather than the vehicle assignment. Vehicle condition terminology also varies across agencies; agency-specific status labels such as the BFP's "Serviceable" and "Unserviceable" classifications are not stored separately and are instead represented by the system's four standardized operational statuses. Passenger and patient details are not recorded by the system, as these fall outside vehicle management scope and avoid storing sensitive personal information. Although the system supports repair logging and vehicle status monitoring, actual repair validation, mechanical inspections, procurement, and external maintenance processes remain outside the system's scope. Vehicles under manufacturer warranty will be marked as Not Operational when damaged or under repair, with the actual repair process remaining dependent on authorized dealers or external service providers. Generated reports are based solely on encoded data and will not produce officially formatted government forms or legally standardized Commission on Audit documents. The study focuses on system development, workflow implementation, and pilot-level testing within the duration of the capstone project.

## CHAPTER 4 — METHODOLOGY

This chapter presents the methodology, results, and discussion of the Rescue Vehicle Management System. It describes the structured approach the proponents followed in determining the needs of the participating agencies, documenting the system requirements, and designing the technical structure of the solution, and it discusses the outputs produced at each stage. The development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System followed the Prototyping Model of the software development life cycle. In this model, the proponents first gather requirements, then design and build a working prototype that the intended users evaluate; the feedback gathered is used to refine the requirements and the prototype across successive iterations until the design is validated, after which the actual system is developed, tested, and deployed. The Prototyping Model was selected because the Rescue Vehicle Management System is being built for four government agencies whose common dispatch and vehicle-maintenance processes had to be reconciled into a single centralized platform — a goal that required continuous feedback and validation from the client agencies rather than a one-time, fixed specification. The proponents conducted two rounds of interviews — an initial requirements interview and a follow-up interview that addressed gaps identified in the first — and developed an interactive prototype that simulates the complete set of workflows. This prototype is being evaluated by the client agencies and the capstone adviser, and their approval and suggestions are incorporated before the application is finalized. This iterative, feedback-driven approach ensures that the resulting system accurately reflects the shared operational needs of the four participating agencies.

Following this model, the development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System was organized into the phases reflected in the project's Schedule of Activities presented in Chapter 3. ### Requirements Analysis

Requirements analysis is the process of determining the needs and expectations of the intended users of the system through close communication with stakeholders, and of resolving conflicts and gaps so that a clear and agreed set of requirements can be established. For the Rescue Vehicle Management System, this phase aimed to identify the operational problems experienced by the four participating rescue agencies in Calbayog City — the Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP), the Philippine National Police (PNP), the City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office (CDRRMO), and the City Health Office (CHO) — and to translate those problems into the specific functions the system must perform to meet the objectives set in Chapter 1.

To gather the requirements, the proponents conducted structured interviews with key personnel from each of the four agencies, including drivers, logistics and supply officers, and operations officers, as well as personnel from the City General Services Office (GSO) Motorpool. The interviews examined how each agency records vehicle and driver information, performs daily vehicle inspections, reports defects and damage, processes repairs and preventive maintenance, logs dispatches, and monitors vehicle availability. In addition to the interviews, the proponents reviewed existing documentation used by the agencies, such as the BFP Motor Vehicle Daily Safety Checklist, the Motor Vehicle Ledger Card, and other manual logbooks and forms, and observed the current vehicle monitoring practices to understand the actual flow of information across personnel and offices. These techniques were used together so that the requirements would be grounded in the real operational workflows of the participating agencies rather than on assumptions.

The gathered data were then analyzed to identify common practices, recurring problems, and gaps that the proposed system needed to address. The analysis revealed that all four agencies maintain vehicle records, driver assignments, license information, vehicle status, and maintenance activities, but that these are managed almost entirely through paper forms, logbooks, spreadsheets, verbal reporting, phone calls, text messages, and group chats. Because this information is scattered across separate records and offices, the agencies cannot readily determine which vehicles are operational, dispatched, under repair, or due for maintenance, and damage reports submitted to the GSO Motorpool are sometimes incomplete. Differences among the agencies were also identified and reconciled during the analysis: only the BFP uses a formal daily checklist following the BLOWBAGETS protocol with two additional items, the Hydraulic System and Fire Pump, while the other agencies rely on informal checks; the CHO follows a rotating, shift-based driver arrangement; and the PNP and CDRRMO already operate limited digital tools that do not cover full fleet maintenance monitoring. These variations were taken into account so that the system could accommodate a standard workflow common to all agencies while still supporting agency-specific needs.

From this analysis, the proponents determined and categorized both the user and system requirements in line with the four objectives of the study: to input vehicle and driver information, to track vehicle maintenance, to provide an administrative module for dispatch and vehicle management, and to generate relevant reports. The requirements were organized into functional capabilities — such as record management, digital inspection, damage reporting, repair and preventive maintenance tracking, dispatch logging, fleet monitoring, report generation, and notifications — and into quality attributes such as performance, security, usability, reliability, and compatibility. This categorized set of requirements served as the Software Requirements Specification that guided the documentation, design, and development of the Rescue Vehicle Management System, and it is presented in detail in the Requirements Documentation section that follows.

| **FR-20** | Report Generation | Allows Agency Administrators to generate filtered, printable reports for inspections, damage reports, repair and maintenance history, preventive maintenance, dispatch logs, and vehicle status summaries; each generated report records the Agency Administrator who generated it and the date of generation. |
| **FR-21** | Notification Services | Delivers in-app notifications to Authorized Drivers for preventive maintenance reminders and vehicle status updates, and to Agency Administrators for new damage reports, new driver access requests, license expiry alerts, and preventive maintenance due alerts. |

Table 3 presents the functional requirements of the Rescue Vehicle Management System, which collectively address the four objectives of the study. The foundational functions — User Authentication (FR-01), Role-Based and Agency-Scoped Access (FR-02), Driver Account Registration and Approval (FR-03), and Self-Service Profile Management (FR-04) — establish secure, role-appropriate access and govern how driver and administrator accounts are created and maintained. The record-management functions (FR-05 to FR-08) fulfill the objective of inputting vehicle and driver information by providing a centralized and searchable replacement for the agencies' separate paper records, while also monitoring driver license validity. The inspection, damage, repair, and preventive maintenance functions (FR-09 to FR-14) fulfill the objective of tracking vehicle maintenance by digitizing the daily BLOWBAGETS checklist, standardizing damage reporting, and maintaining complete repair and maintenance histories. The dispatch, monitoring, and status functions (FR-15 to FR-19) fulfill the objective of providing an administrative module for dispatch and vehicle management by giving administrators an immediate, consolidated view of fleet readiness across a single shared status field. The report generation function (FR-20) fulfills the objective of generating relevant reports, while the notification function (FR-21) acts as an enabling function across all four objectives by keeping both user roles informed of time-sensitive events such as new damage reports, license expiry, and due preventive maintenance. Together, these functional requirements form the basis for the system's design and guide the subsequent development and testing phases of the project.

#### Non-Functional Requirements

The non-functional requirements describe the quality attributes of the Rescue Vehicle Management System. Unlike the functional requirements, which specify what the system must do, the non-functional requirements define how well the system must perform under different conditions to be effective, secure, and usable for rescue vehicle operations. These requirements were identified from the operational context of the participating agencies, particularly the need for reliable records, the separation of data among agencies, and the use of the system by field personnel who are not necessarily regular technology users. The non-functional requirements of the Rescue Vehicle Management System are presented in Table 4.

**Table 4. Non-Functional Requirements for the Rescue Vehicle Management System**

| Code | Non-Functional Requirement | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **NFR-01** | Performance | The system should respond promptly to user actions, loading dashboards, record lists, and reports without significant delay, even when multiple agency users are working at the same time. |
| **NFR-02** | Security | The system should protect data through authenticated access, hashed passwords, role-based authorization, agency-level data isolation, and encrypted communication so that each agency's records remain private. |

Table 4 presents the non-functional requirements of the Rescue Vehicle Management System, covering the quality standards the system must meet to support its intended users. Performance (NFR-01) keeps dashboards, record lists, and reports responsive even when multiple agency users access the system at the same time, while reliability (NFR-04) keeps vehicle status consistent across both platforms and notifications delivered for time-sensitive events such as preventive maintenance and license expiry. Security (NFR-02) protects each agency's records through authenticated access, hashed passwords, role-based authorization, agency-level data isolation, and encrypted communication. Usability (NFR-03) keeps the interface simple and consistent for drivers and administrators completing routine tasks, with large touch targets suited to mobile use in the field. Together with the functional requirements, these quality attributes guided the architectural and design decisions discussed in the following section.

### Design of Software, Systems, Product, and/or Processes

This section presents the technical design of the Rescue Vehicle Management System and the structures involved in achieving that design in accordance with the documented requirements. It describes, through diagrams and supporting narrative, how the system was organized so that its components work together to satisfy both the functional and non-functional requirements established in the preceding sections. The design is presented in three parts: the system architecture, which provides the foundational blueprint of the system; the process model, comprising the context diagram, data flow diagram, and functional decomposition diagram; and the data model, comprising the entity-relationship diagram and data dictionary.

#### System Architecture

The system architecture serves as the overall structure of the Rescue Vehicle Management System, illustrating how users, applications, the centralized database, and the notification service interact to support vehicle monitoring, inspection, maintenance, and dispatch management. The architecture is composed of two user roles, namely the Authorized Driver and the Agency Administrator, who access the system through their respective platforms. A centralized database is used to store and manage all vehicle-related records, while Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) is integrated to deliver real-time notifications to drivers. This architecture supports centralized data management and allows both mobile and web platforms to access consistent and updated information. The system architecture of the Rescue Vehicle Management System is shown in Figure 2.

**Figure 2. System Architecture of the Rescue Vehicle Management System**
*(See system architecture diagram in the original document.)*

As illustrated in Figure 2, the Authorized Driver interacts with the system through the Driver Mobile Application. Through the mobile application, drivers can submit digital BLOWBAGETS inspection results and damage reports to the centralized database. In return, the mobile application retrieves vehicle information, vehicle status, and other relevant records stored in the database. The mobile application also serves as the platform where drivers receive notifications related to vehicle status updates and preventive maintenance schedules.

The Agency Administrator accesses the system through the Web Dashboard. Using the dashboard, administrators manage vehicle records, driver records, dispatch logs, repair records, and preventive maintenance information. Any records created or updated through the dashboard are stored in the centralized database. Likewise, the dashboard retrieves records from the database to support monitoring activities, vehicle status tracking, and report generation.

The centralized database functions as the core repository of the system. It stores all information related to vehicles, drivers, inspections, damage reports, dispatch logs, repair histories, and preventive maintenance schedules — the records from which the system's reports are generated. Both the Driver Mobile Application and the Web Dashboard communicate with the database through the system's Laravel backend, which processes all requests and enforces role-based, agency-scoped access, ensuring that all users access the same set of updated records. This centralized approach minimizes duplicate records and supports consistent monitoring across participating agencies.

The architecture also integrates Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) to support the notification component of the system. When specific events occur within the database, such as preventive maintenance schedules becoming due or vehicle status updates being made by an administrator, notification requests are triggered and sent to FCM. FCM then delivers the corresponding push notifications to the Driver Mobile Application. This mechanism enables drivers to receive important updates without manually checking the system.

Overall, the architecture provides a centralized environment for managing rescue vehicle operations. By connecting the Driver Mobile Application, Web Dashboard, Database, and Firebase Cloud Messaging service, the system supports digital inspections, defect reporting, maintenance monitoring, dispatch logging, vehicle status monitoring, and real-time notifications within a unified platform.

#### Entity-Relationship Diagram and Data Dictionary

The data model defines the database structure that supports the functional requirements. The RVMS database comprises the domain tables agencies, users (administrators and drivers, distinguished by role), vehicles, inspection_checklist_items, inspections, inspection_items, damage_reports, repair_logs, pm_schedules, dispatches, and notifications, together with the standard Laravel/Sanctum framework tables. Every operational table carries an agency_id foreign key that enforces agency-scoped access (FR-02); the four vehicle operational statuses live as a single field on the vehicles table (FR-18); and configurable thresholds (license-expiry warning days; PM Due-Soon thresholds) are stored as columns rather than constants. An agency can have many users, vehicles, inspections, damage reports, repair logs, preventive maintenance schedules, dispatches, and notifications, while each of these records belongs to exactly one agency; this enforces the agency-scoped access of the system. A user is either an Agency Administrator or an Authorized Driver. Each vehicle is assigned to one primary driver, and a driver may be the assigned driver of one or more vehicles. Each vehicle can have many inspections, damage reports, repair logs, preventive maintenance schedules, and dispatches, and each of these records refers to exactly one vehicle. Every inspection, damage report, repair log, and dispatch also records the driver who carried it out, while inspections and damage reports additionally record the administrator who reviewed them. An inspection is composed of several inspection items, and each inspection item corresponds to one item from the inspection checklist catalog. Finally, each notification is addressed to a single user. The complete entity-relationship diagram and the column-level data dictionary are maintained in the approved database design (see CLAUDE.md) and in the manuscript's Chapter 4.
